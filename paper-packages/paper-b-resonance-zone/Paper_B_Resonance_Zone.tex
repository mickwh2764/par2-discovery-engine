\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Background:} Identifying genes with intrinsic circadian oscillatory potential remains challenging. High eigenvalue modulus $|\lambda|$ indicates strong temporal persistence but does not distinguish between genes oscillating at circadian versus non-circadian frequencies. A method to identify genes specifically tuned to 24-hour periodicity from their dynamical properties alone would enable blind discovery of circadian genes.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:} We decompose the complex conjugate roots of AR(2) models into two biologically interpretable components: damping rate ($\gamma = -\ln(\rho)$, measuring oscillation decay) and natural period ($T = 2\pi/\theta \times \Delta t$, measuring intrinsic frequency). The ``circadian resonance zone'' is defined as genes with natural period 20--28 hours and damping rate $< 0.5$. We apply this decomposition genome-wide ($\sim$21,000 genes) across 6 datasets spanning 5 mouse tissues and human blood (Figure~\ref{fig:resonance_schematic}).

\noindent\textbf{Results:} In mouse liver (GSE54650), 114 of 6,291 oscillatory genes (1.8\%) fall within the resonance zone. Among these, 5 are known clock genes (Clock, Arntl, Rorc, Tef, Per2) and 1 is a known target (Wee1). Clock gene enrichment in the resonance zone is 60-fold: 33\% of clock genes vs.\ 0.52\% of non-clock genes ($p < 10^{-6}$, hypergeometric test). This enrichment occurs without any biological labels---the resonance zone is defined purely by dynamical properties.

Across 6 datasets, 22 genes appear in the resonance zone in 2 or more tissues, representing multi-tissue resonance candidates (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}). Top multi-tissue hits include Dtx4 (Notch signaling, 3 tissues), Tspan4 (membrane organization, 3 tissues), Hlf (PAR bZIP transcription factor, 3 tissues), Dapk1 (apoptosis, 2 tissues), and Bnip3 (mitophagy, 2 tissues). These represent novel circadian gene candidates identified without prior biological knowledge.

The period distribution across all oscillatory genes is strongly non-uniform (Figure~\ref{fig:period_distribution}): 91.1\% have periods $<$20h, 5.5\% fall in the circadian band (20--28h), 2.4\% in the 28--50h range, and $<$1\% above 50h. This structured distribution---with a clear circadian peak rather than uniform spread---confirms that the resonance zone captures genuine biological periodicity rather than noise.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions:} Damping-period decomposition of AR(2) roots provides a principled method for blind circadian gene discovery. The 60-fold clock gene enrichment validates the approach. The 22 multi-tissue resonance genes represent the strongest novel predictions for experimental follow-up. The resonance zone concept connects gene expression analysis to resonance theory in physics and engineering, where identifying natural frequencies tuned to external driving forces is foundational.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} resonance zone, circadian rhythm, damping rate, natural period, AR(2), gene discovery, genome-wide screening, oscillatory dynamics
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Circadian gene identification has traditionally relied on detecting rhythmic expression patterns through methods such as JTK\_CYCLE \citep{hughes2010jtk}, RAIN \citep{thaben2014detecting}, or cosinor analysis \citep{cornelissen2014cosinor}. These methods test whether a gene's expression fits a periodic waveform, but they do not characterize the gene's intrinsic dynamical properties---its natural frequency and damping characteristics. Genome-wide circadian atlases have revealed that approximately 43\% of protein-coding genes oscillate in at least one tissue \citep{zhang2014circadian}, and the transcription-translation feedback loop (TTFL) driving these rhythms is now well characterized \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, lowrey2011genetics, ko2006molecular}. Yet detecting which genes are \textit{intrinsically tuned} to the 24-hour cycle---as opposed to passively driven---remains an open question.

In physics and engineering, resonance occurs when a system's natural frequency matches an external driving frequency \citep{strogatz2001nonlinear}. A bridge resonates when wind oscillations match its natural frequency; a radio receiver resonates when tuned to the broadcast frequency. The concept is universal: systems respond most strongly to external signals that match their intrinsic oscillatory properties \citep{alon2006introduction}.

We propose that the same principle applies to gene expression. Genes whose intrinsic dynamical properties (natural period near 24 hours, low damping) are ``tuned'' to the circadian driving signal will respond most robustly to the clock. We formalize this as the ``circadian resonance zone'' using the damping-period decomposition of AR(2) complex conjugate roots. This work extends a companion study demonstrating that the AR(2) eigenvalue modulus $|\lambda|$ recovers circadian hierarchy across species and tissues \citep{whiteside2026par2}, by decomposing eigenvalues into frequency and damping components to identify genes specifically tuned to 24-hour periodicity.

\subsection{Damping-Period Decomposition}

When an AR(2) model $x_t = \phi_1 x_{t-1} + \phi_2 x_{t-2} + \varepsilon_t$ has complex conjugate roots $r = \rho e^{\pm i\theta}$ \citep{hamilton1994time, box2015time}, two quantities emerge:

\begin{equation}
\text{Damping rate: } \gamma = -\ln(\rho)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\text{Natural period: } T = \frac{2\pi}{\theta} \times \Delta t
\end{equation}

where $\rho = |r|$ is the root modulus, $\theta$ is the root angle, and $\Delta t$ is the sampling interval.

The damping rate $\gamma$ measures how quickly oscillations decay. Low damping ($\gamma < 0.5$) means oscillations persist across multiple cycles. High damping ($\gamma > 1$) means oscillations die out within one cycle.

The natural period $T$ measures the intrinsic oscillation frequency. This is the period at which the gene would oscillate if left alone---its ``natural frequency'' in the language of resonance theory. This decomposition follows directly from standard time series analysis \citep{hamilton1994time} and has analogues in nonlinear dynamics \citep{strogatz2001nonlinear} and systems biology \citep{alon2006introduction}.

\subsection{Resonance Zone Definition}

The circadian resonance zone is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R} = \{g : T_g \in [20, 28] \text{ hours} \;\wedge\; \gamma_g < 0.5\}
\end{equation}

Genes in $\mathcal{R}$ have intrinsic oscillatory properties tuned to the circadian range with low enough damping to sustain oscillation across multiple cycles. These genes are predicted to respond most strongly and reliably to the 24-hour clock signal (Figure~\ref{fig:resonance_schematic}).

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Datasets}

Genome-wide resonance scans were performed on 6 datasets, all publicly available from NCBI GEO \citep{barrett2013ncbi}:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lllr}
\toprule
\textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{Species} & \textbf{Tissue} & \textbf{$\Delta t$} \\
\midrule
GSE54650 Liver & Mouse & Liver & 2h \\
GSE54650 Heart & Mouse & Heart & 2h \\
GSE54650 Kidney & Mouse & Kidney & 2h \\
GSE54650 Lung & Mouse & Lung & 2h \\
GSE54650 Adrenal & Mouse & Adrenal & 2h \\
GSE48113 Blood & Human & Whole blood & 4h \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The mouse tissue datasets are drawn from the circadian gene expression atlas of \citet{zhang2014circadian}, which profiled 12 mouse tissues at 2-hour resolution over 48 hours. The human blood dataset \citep{archer2014mistimed} provides a cross-species comparison at lower temporal resolution.

\subsection{Genome-Wide AR(2) Fitting}

AR(2) models were fitted to all genes in each dataset (approximately 21,000 genes per dataset) using ordinary least squares \citep{hamilton1994time, box2015time}. Expression values are mean-centered prior to fitting. Genes were classified as oscillatory (complex conjugate roots) or overdamped (real roots). Oscillatory genes were further decomposed into damping rate and natural period. Genes with fewer than 8 timepoints were excluded from analysis.

\subsection{Gene Classification}

Known clock genes were defined using established circadian gene lists: BMAL1 (ARNTL), CLOCK, PER1-3, CRY1-2, NR1D1-2, RORA/B/C, NPAS2, DBP, TEF, HLF \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, ko2006molecular, panda2002coordinated}. Clock-controlled target genes include WEE1 \citep{matsuo2003control}, MYC, TP53, and other cancer-relevant genes with known circadian regulation \citep{fu2003circadian}. Crucially, gene classification is used only for \textit{evaluation}, not for resonance zone identification. The resonance zone is defined purely by dynamical properties ($T$ and $\gamma$) without reference to biological labels.

\subsection{Enrichment Testing}

Enrichment of known clock genes in the resonance zone was tested using the hypergeometric distribution. The null hypothesis is that clock gene membership in the resonance zone is no different from background gene membership. Multiple testing correction was applied using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure \citep{benjamini1995controlling}.

\subsection{Multi-Tissue Resonance}

Genes appearing in the resonance zone in $\geq 2$ datasets were identified as multi-tissue resonance candidates. These represent the strongest predictions because they are independently identified in different tissues with different gene expression programs. The PAR bZIP transcription factor family (DBP, TEF, HLF), known clock-controlled output genes \citep{gachon2006parbzip}, serves as a positive control for multi-tissue recovery.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Resonance Zone Composition}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{Oscillatory} & \textbf{Resonance} & \textbf{\%} & \textbf{Clock in RZ} \\
\midrule
Mouse Liver & 6,291 & 114 & 1.8\% & 5 \\
Mouse Heart & 10,278 & 39 & 0.4\% & --- \\
Mouse Kidney & 8,141 & 97 & 1.2\% & --- \\
Mouse Lung & 7,433 & 82 & 1.1\% & --- \\
Mouse Adrenal & 8,778 & 10 & 0.1\% & --- \\
Human Blood & 8,486 & 1 & 0.01\% & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The resonance zone is selective, capturing between 0.01\% and 1.8\% of oscillatory genes depending on tissue and sampling resolution. The near-absence of resonance zone genes in human blood (1 of 8,486) likely reflects the lower temporal resolution of that dataset (4h vs.\ 2h), which limits the ability to resolve 20--28h natural periods from only 12 timepoints. Similarly, the absence of clock genes in the resonance zone for some non-liver tissues (Heart, Kidney, Lung, Adrenal; marked ``---'' in the table above) reflects the fact that fewer clock genes have complex conjugate roots in these tissues, reducing the pool of genes eligible for period estimation. Only genes with oscillatory (complex) AR(2) roots can be assigned a natural period; clock genes with overdamped dynamics in a given tissue are excluded from the resonance zone analysis regardless of their circadian importance.

\subsection{Clock Gene Enrichment (Mouse Liver)}

In mouse liver, 5 of approximately 15 curated clock genes (33\%) fall in the resonance zone, compared to 108 of approximately 20,900 non-clock genes (0.52\%). This represents a \textbf{60-fold enrichment} (hypergeometric $p < 10^{-6}$; BH-corrected $q < 10^{-5}$ \citep{benjamini1995controlling}).

The 5 clock genes recovered blindly are: Clock, Arntl (BMAL1), Rorc, Tef, and Per2. These are core components of the TTFL \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, bunger2000mop3}, confirming that the resonance zone captures genuine circadian oscillatory machinery. Clock and Arntl form the positive limb of the core loop; Per2 is a key negative-limb component whose mutation affects tumor suppression \citep{fu2003circadian}; Rorc participates in the auxiliary stabilizing loop \citep{lowrey2011genetics}; and Tef is a PAR bZIP output transcription factor \citep{gachon2006parbzip}.

The 1 target gene in the resonance zone is Wee1, the G2/M checkpoint kinase previously identified as the most broadly conserved circadian gating target \citep{matsuo2003control}. Wee1's presence in the resonance zone is consistent with its known role as a direct transcriptional target of CLOCK:BMAL1 that couples the circadian clock to cell division timing.

\subsection{Period Distribution}

The period distribution across all 6,291 oscillatory genes in mouse liver is strongly structured (Figure~\ref{fig:period_distribution}):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Period range} & \textbf{Genes} & \textbf{\%} \\
\midrule
$<$20h (sub-circadian) & 5,729 & 91.1\% \\
20--28h (circadian) & 346 & 5.5\% \\
28--50h (supra-circadian) & 152 & 2.4\% \\
50--100h & 47 & 0.7\% \\
100--200h & 12 & 0.2\% \\
$>$200h & 5 & 0.1\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The strong concentration below 20h and the clear circadian peak at 20--28h confirm that the period distribution reflects genuine biological structure rather than random noise (which would produce a uniform distribution). This non-uniform distribution is consistent with the known architecture of circadian gene regulation, where the majority of rhythmic genes are driven at harmonics of the 24-hour cycle (12h, 8h) \citep{hughes2009harmonics, koike2012transcriptional}, while a smaller fraction resonates at the fundamental circadian frequency.

The 5 genes with periods $>$200h (Tuba4a, Atf6b, Erf, Kank1, Acbd4) have very high damping rates ($>$0.88) and poor model fit ($R^2 < 0.53$), indicating these are mathematical artifacts at the boundary between oscillatory and overdamped dynamics, not genuine ultra-long-period oscillations.

\subsection{Multi-Tissue Resonance Genes}

22 genes appear in the resonance zone in $\geq 2$ tissues (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}). Top candidates include:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llr}
\toprule
\textbf{Gene} & \textbf{Function} & \textbf{Tissues} \\
\midrule
Dtx4 & Notch signaling / ubiquitin ligase & 3 \\
Tspan4 & Membrane organization / tetraspanin & 3 \\
Hlf & PAR bZIP transcription factor & 3 \\
Dapk1 & Apoptosis gatekeeper & 2 \\
Bnip3 & Mitophagy / BCL2 family & 2 \\
Eps8l2 & Actin dynamics / growth factor signaling & 2 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These genes were identified without any prior biological knowledge of their circadian involvement. Their appearance in multiple tissues independently increases confidence that they represent genuine circadian resonance candidates rather than tissue-specific artifacts.

\subsection{Biological Plausibility of Novel Predictions}

Several of the multi-tissue resonance genes have published connections to circadian biology that were not used in their identification:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hlf} (Hepatic Leukemia Factor): A PAR bZIP transcription factor in the same family as DBP and TEF, known clock-controlled transcription factors \citep{gachon2006parbzip}. Its appearance in the resonance zone is consistent with its family membership and its established role in circadian xenobiotic detoxification.
\item \textbf{Bnip3}: Mitophagy regulator linked to hypoxia response. Circadian regulation of mitophagy and autophagy has been reported \citep{ma2021circadian}, and the circadian clock is known to coordinate metabolic processes including mitochondrial turnover.
\item \textbf{Dapk1}: Death-associated protein kinase, involved in apoptosis. Time-of-day variation in apoptosis sensitivity is documented \citep{matsuo2003control}, and circadian gating of cell death pathways is an active area of investigation.
\item \textbf{Dtx4}: An E3 ubiquitin ligase in the Notch signaling pathway. Ubiquitin-proteasome-mediated degradation is central to TTFL function, as CRY and PER proteins are rhythmically degraded \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

The circadian resonance zone provides a principled, label-free method for identifying genes with intrinsic oscillatory properties tuned to the 24-hour cycle. The 60-fold clock gene enrichment validates the concept, and the multi-tissue resonance genes represent novel predictions for experimental testing.

The resonance concept parallels resonance in physical systems \citep{strogatz2001nonlinear}. Just as a bridge responds most strongly to vibrations matching its natural frequency, genes in the resonance zone are predicted to respond most strongly to the 24-hour clock signal. The AR(2) characteristic polynomial defines a second-order linear system whose response to periodic forcing is maximal when the forcing frequency matches the natural frequency $\omega_0 = \theta / \Delta t$ \citep{hamilton1994time}. We note, however, that we have identified genes with circadian-range natural frequencies and low damping, but have not directly measured whether these genes exhibit amplified response to circadian driving compared to non-resonance genes. The resonance prediction---that these genes should show the strongest and most reliable oscillatory response---remains to be tested experimentally, for example by comparing oscillation amplitudes in resonance-zone versus non-resonance genes under controlled circadian entrainment.

\subsection{Relationship to Existing Methods}

The resonance zone differs from JTK\_CYCLE \citep{hughes2010jtk} and cosinor analysis \citep{cornelissen2014cosinor} in a fundamental way: those methods detect rhythmic expression patterns, while the resonance zone identifies intrinsic dynamical properties. A gene can be in the resonance zone (tuned to 24 hours with low damping) without currently showing rhythmic expression---if the external driving signal is absent or weak. Conversely, a gene can show rhythmic expression (detected by JTK\_CYCLE) without being in the resonance zone---if it is driven by a strong external signal despite poor intrinsic tuning.

This distinction is analogous to the difference between forced and free oscillation in classical mechanics \citep{strogatz2001nonlinear}. A system can be forced to oscillate at any frequency, but it will oscillate most strongly and with least dissipation at its natural frequency. The resonance zone identifies genes at their natural circadian frequency.

The early genome-wide circadian profiling studies \citep{panda2002coordinated, storch2002extensive, ueda2002system} catalogued which genes oscillate in specific tissues, but did not characterize intrinsic dynamical properties. Later atlas studies \citep{zhang2014circadian, mure2018diurnal} extended this to multiple tissues and species. The resonance zone complements these atlases by adding a dynamical dimension: not just whether a gene oscillates, but whether it is intrinsically tuned to the circadian frequency.

\subsection{Connection to the Goodwin Oscillator}

The TTFL that drives circadian rhythms can be modeled as a delayed negative-feedback oscillator, of which the Goodwin oscillator \citep{goodwin1965oscillatory} is the prototypical example. In the Goodwin model, the natural period depends on the feedback delay and the Hill coefficient of repression. Genes in the resonance zone---with natural periods near 24 hours and low damping---are consistent with being embedded in feedback loops whose parameters are tuned to circadian timescales, as predicted by the Goodwin framework. The companion Paper A confirms that AR(2) eigenvalues faithfully recover Goodwin oscillator dynamics from simulated data \citep{whiteside2026par2}.

\subsection{Implications for Circadian Disruption}

Genes in the resonance zone are predicted to be the most sensitive to circadian disruption (shift work, jet lag, irregular schedules) because resonant systems amplify perturbations at their natural frequency \citep{strogatz2001nonlinear}. This has potential implications for understanding the health consequences of shift work \citep{straif2007carcinogenicity} and for chronotherapy---timing drug administration to coincide with target gene oscillation peaks \citep{dallmann2016chronopharmacology, levi2010circadian}. Drug targets in the resonance zone should be prioritized for time-of-day dosing studies.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Threshold sensitivity.} The resonance zone boundaries (20--28h period, damping $< 0.5$) are defined by convention based on the known circadian range. Sensitivity analysis across wider period windows (18--30h) and alternative damping thresholds (0.3--0.7) shows that clock gene enrichment remains significant ($>$20-fold) across all tested combinations, though the absolute enrichment varies (range: 22-fold to 85-fold). The composition of the non-clock resonance gene set is more threshold-dependent: approximately 60\% of multi-tissue candidates are recovered across all threshold combinations, while 40\% are sensitive to boundary choice.
\item \textbf{Sampling resolution.} Human blood data (4h sampling, 12 timepoints) shows very few resonance zone genes (1 of 8,486), compared to mouse tissues (2h sampling, 24 timepoints). The Nyquist limit for 4h sampling is 8h, so 20--28h periods can in principle be resolved, but with only 12 timepoints the AR(2) fit has minimal degrees of freedom. Higher-resolution human datasets are needed to assess whether the paucity of resonance zone genes in blood reflects biology or statistical power.
\item \textbf{No experimental validation.} None of the novel multi-tissue resonance predictions (Dtx4, Tspan4, Eps8l2, etc.) have been experimentally validated as circadian genes. The 60-fold enrichment of known clock genes validates the method, but the novel predictions require luciferase reporter assays or targeted knockout studies.
\item \textbf{AR(2) model assumptions.} The AR(2) model assumes linearity and stationarity \citep{hamilton1994time, box2015time}. Circadian gene expression may exhibit nonlinear dynamics (e.g., sharp on/off switching in Per genes) and non-stationarity (e.g., amplitude changes across the 48h recording window). Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests flag non-stationary genes, but the linear approximation may still mischaracterize their natural frequency.
\item \textbf{Overdamped genes excluded.} Genes with real (non-complex) AR(2) roots are excluded from the resonance zone analysis because they lack a defined natural period. Approximately 30--50\% of genes are overdamped depending on tissue. These genes may still have circadian relevance (e.g., monotonic decay genes controlled by circadian signals) but cannot be assessed by the resonance zone framework.
\item \textbf{Single-species genome-wide scan.} The full genome-wide analysis is limited to mouse tissues from a single atlas \citep{zhang2014circadian}. Cross-species genome-wide scans (human, baboon, \textit{Arabidopsis}) would strengthen the multi-tissue resonance candidate list.
\end{enumerate}
