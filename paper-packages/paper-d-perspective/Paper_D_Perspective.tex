\begin{abstract}
\noindent The eigenvalue modulus $|\lambda|$ has been the universal language of dynamical systems for over 200 years. It determines energy levels in quantum mechanics, natural frequencies in vibration analysis, stability in control theory, growth rates in ecology, persistence timescales in economics, and memory in climate models. Here we argue that the eigenvalue modulus of a second-order autoregressive model applied to gene expression time series measures a biologically meaningful quantity: \textit{temporal persistence}, or how much a gene's past determines its future. We present evidence from 35 datasets across 4 species that this single metric blindly recovers known circadian hierarchy, is partially independent of network connectivity and chromatin state, converges with four independent research programs, and reproduces the dynamical properties of five canonical ODE models from biology. We propose that temporal persistence is an underappreciated axis of biological variation---distinct from expression level, connectivity, or epigenetic state---and that the eigenvalue provides a direct measurement of this property using the same mathematics that has proven foundational across physical, engineering, and social sciences.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} eigenvalue modulus, temporal persistence, gene expression memory, dynamical systems, circadian hierarchy, cross-disciplinary, autoregressive model
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: The Most Universal Number in Science}

In 1904, David Hilbert posed a question that would shape modern mathematics: given a linear transformation, what are its characteristic values? The answer---eigenvalues---became the foundation of every field that studies dynamical systems.

The word ``eigenvalue'' comes from the German \textit{eigen}, meaning ``own'' or ``characteristic.'' An eigenvalue is literally the system's own number: the quantity that tells you how the system naturally behaves when left alone. Whether the system is an atom (Schr\"{o}dinger, 1926), a bridge (vibration analysis), an economy \citep{sims1980macroeconomics}, or a climate \citep{hasselmann1976stochastic}, the eigenvalue answers the same question: \textit{does the system grow, decay, or oscillate, and how fast?}

We propose that this same question---applied to gene expression---reveals fundamental biological organization. The mammalian circadian clock orchestrates rhythmic gene expression across virtually all tissues, with approximately 43\% of protein-coding genes showing circadian oscillation in at least one tissue \citep{zhang2014circadian}. The core transcription-translation feedback loop (TTFL) creates a hierarchical architecture: core clock genes drive thousands of downstream clock-controlled genes \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional}. Yet no single quantitative metric has previously captured this hierarchy from expression data alone.

Here we argue that the eigenvalue modulus of a second-order autoregressive (AR(2)) model---a tool borrowed from time-series econometrics \citep{hamilton1994time, granger1969investigating}---provides exactly such a metric. The AR(2) model:
\begin{equation}
x_t = \phi_1 x_{t-1} + \phi_2 x_{t-2} + \varepsilon_t
\end{equation}
fits two coefficients to a gene's expression time series, and the eigenvalue modulus derived from the characteristic polynomial $z^2 - \phi_1 z - \phi_2 = 0$ measures temporal persistence: how strongly a gene's past expression determines its future. This approach connects gene expression dynamics to a mathematical framework refined across physics, engineering, economics, and climate science over two centuries \citep{strogatz2001nonlinear}.

\subsection{The Common Thread}

In every field, the eigenvalue modulus tells you one thing: \textbf{how much the system remembers}. High eigenvalue $\rightarrow$ strong memory $\rightarrow$ hard to disrupt. Low eigenvalue $\rightarrow$ weak memory $\rightarrow$ easily pushed around. This interpretation is grounded in the formal theory of autoregressive processes \citep{hamilton1994time, box2015time} and extends naturally to systems biology \citep{alon2006introduction, stelling2004robustness}.

The stationarity boundary ($|\lambda| = 1$) means the same thing everywhere:
\begin{itemize}
\item $|\lambda| < 1$: Perturbations decay. The system forgets.
\item $|\lambda| = 1$: Perturbations persist indefinitely. Perfect memory.
\item $|\lambda| > 1$: Perturbations grow. The system is unstable.
\end{itemize}

\section{Evidence That Eigenvalue Measures Biological Memory}

\subsection{Blind Hierarchy Recovery}

The AR(2) eigenvalue modulus, computed without any biological labels, recovers the known circadian hierarchy established by decades of molecular clock research \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, panda2002coordinated, ko2006molecular}:

\begin{equation}
|\lambda|_{\text{clock}} > |\lambda|_{\text{target}} > |\lambda|_{\text{other}}
\end{equation}

across 12/12 mouse tissues, 3/3 human blood conditions, 3/3 \textit{Arabidopsis} replicates, and 4/8 baboon tissues (with bootstrap-significant gap), spanning 4 species \citep{whiteside2026par2a}. Gene classification follows established circadian gene lists \citep{panda2002coordinated, storch2002extensive, ko2006molecular}, but is used only for \textit{evaluation}, not for model fitting. This means genes that biologists have spent decades classifying as ``core clock'' happen to have the highest temporal persistence. The AR(2) model recovers this classification from two coefficients.

The 12-tissue mouse atlas \citep{zhang2014circadian} provides the most comprehensive test, with the hierarchy preserved across liver, kidney, heart, lung, muscle, brain, and six other tissues. Human blood data \citep{hughes2010jtk} and baboon tissue data \citep{mure2018diurnal} extend this finding across primates.

\subsection{Partial Independence from Other Metrics}

If eigenvalue merely recapitulated existing biological metrics, it would add nothing new. We tested $|\lambda|$ against four independent metrics (Figure~\ref{fig:independence}):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrp{5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Comparison} & \textbf{Spearman $\rho$} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
\midrule
$|\lambda|$ vs. STRING network degree & $-0.29$ & Weak negative; high-memory genes are not highly connected \\
$|\lambda|$ vs. cosinor amplitude & $0.69$ & Moderate; oscillation strength partially correlates \\
$|\lambda|$ vs. H3K4me3 chromatin & $0.08$ & Near-zero; temporal memory is independent of chromatin marks \citep{koike2012transcriptional} \\
$|\lambda|$ vs. AR(2) $R^2$ & $0.83$ & Expected; model fit correlates with signal strength \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The key finding: \textbf{eigenvalue is nearly independent of chromatin state ($\rho = 0.08$)} and weakly anti-correlated with network connectivity ($\rho = -0.29$). This means temporal persistence is not a proxy for being ``important'' (highly connected) or ``active'' (open chromatin). It measures something distinct: how strongly the gene's past constrains its future. The near-zero correlation with H3K4me3 is particularly striking, given that chromatin state is often assumed to determine expression dynamics \citep{heard2014transgenerational, koike2012transcriptional}. Multiple comparisons were corrected using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure \citep{benjamini1995controlling}.

\subsection{ODE Model Convergence}

Five canonical ODE models from biology---when simulated, discretized, and fitted with AR(2)---produce eigenvalues consistent with their known dynamics \citep{whiteside2026par2a}. The Goodwin oscillator \citep{goodwin1965oscillatory}, Leloup-Goldbeter circadian clock, FitzHugh-Nagumo excitable system, Lotka-Volterra predator-prey, and Tyson-Novak cell cycle models all yield AR(2) eigenvalues matching their Jacobian-derived values with $\sim$80\% accuracy. This confirms that the AR(2) eigenvalue faithfully captures the underlying dynamical regime, not a statistical artifact of the fitting procedure.

\subsection{Convergence with Independent Research Programs}

The eigenvalue framework converges with four independently developed research programs:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Boman Lab} (cell division timing): 5 qualitative convergence points. Boman's work on colonic crypt dynamics \citep{boman2025biolcell} uses Fibonacci-structured cell division models whose predictions are consistent with the persistence hierarchy the AR(2) eigenvalue recovers.
\item \textbf{Takahashi/Hogenesch} (circadian canon): 6 qualitative convergence points. The TTFL architecture described by Takahashi \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional} predicts exactly the clock $>$ target hierarchy that the eigenvalue blindly recovers.
\item \textbf{Five Biological Rules} (biological organization): 5 qualitative convergence points.
\item \textbf{Waddington Landscape} (epigenetic topology): 5 qualitative convergence points. The Waddington landscape metaphor predicts that stable cell states correspond to attractors---i.e., high persistence \citep{heard2014transgenerational}.
\end{enumerate}

We stress that these convergences are qualitative assessments of conceptual alignment between independently developed frameworks, not formal statistical tests. They emerged when comparing PAR(2) predictions against published results from research programs with no connection to autoregressive modeling, and are presented as suggestive rather than definitive.

\section{Why Gene Expression Has Memory}

The biological basis of temporal persistence is multi-generational cellular memory. When a cell divides, its daughter cells inherit not just DNA sequence but also:

\begin{itemize}
\item Chromatin states (histone modifications, DNA methylation) \citep{heard2014transgenerational}
\item Transcription factor concentrations
\item mRNA and protein half-lives
\item Metabolite pools
\end{itemize}

These inherited states create autocorrelation in gene expression: what the cell expressed yesterday influences what it expresses today. Single-cell studies have confirmed that individual fibroblasts maintain persistent, independently phased circadian rhythms \citep{welsh2004bioluminescence}, and that these rhythms can be quantified at the single-cell level \citep{westermark2009quantification}---demonstrating that expression autocorrelation is a genuine cellular property, not a population-averaging artifact. The AR(2) model captures temporal memory with two lags---expression at time $t$ depends on expression at $t-1$ and $t-2$.

The AR(2) model is preferred over AR(1) and AR(3) for $>$70\% of genes by AIC/BIC \citep{whiteside2026par2a}. While it is tempting to interpret the two-lag structure as corresponding to two cell cycles of memory, this mapping depends on the sampling interval relative to cell cycle duration, which varies across tissues and conditions. The preference for AR(2) over AR(1) is more conservatively interpreted as reflecting oscillatory dynamics (which require at least two parameters to capture) rather than a specific biological memory depth.

\section{Why This Matters}

If temporal persistence is a genuine biological property, it has immediate implications:

\subsection{Drug Targeting}

Genes with high $|\lambda|$ (strong memory) are hard to perturb with drugs---they bounce back. Genes with low $|\lambda|$ (weak memory) are easy to push around. This predicts that:
\begin{itemize}
\item Drugs targeting high-$|\lambda|$ genes need sustained dosing
\item Drugs targeting genes in the resonance zone should be timed to the circadian cycle \citep{dallmann2016chronopharmacology, matsuo2003control}
\item Combination therapies may need to target both a gene and its memory mechanism
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Aging (Speculative)}

We speculate that if aging erodes temporal persistence---i.e., genes lose regulatory memory with age---then the eigenvalue could in principle serve as a dynamical aging clock, complementary to epigenetic clocks that measure methylation age \citep{horvath2013dna}. Preliminary observations from age-stratified datasets suggest that eigenvalue distributions may shift with age \citep{whiteside2026par2a}, but this hypothesis requires dedicated longitudinal cohort studies with matched young and old samples before any conclusion can be drawn.

\subsection{Disease (Speculative)}

Cancer, neurodegeneration, and metabolic syndrome all involve circadian disruption \citep{fu2003circadian, masri2015circadian}. The eigenvalue could in principle provide a per-gene, per-tissue measure of how much temporal organization has been lost. However, this application remains entirely hypothetical: no disease-specific eigenvalue analysis has been performed, and the predictions about which genes are most disrupted require disease-specific time-series datasets that are not yet available.

\section{The Minimal Model Argument}

Perhaps the most striking aspect of this work is the simplicity of the model. Two coefficients ($\phi_1$, $\phi_2$) fitted by ordinary least squares \citep{hamilton1994time}. No deep learning, no complex priors, no multi-parameter optimization. The eigenvalue modulus derived from these two numbers recovers:

\begin{itemize}
\item Clock $>$ target hierarchy across 4 species
\item Nine-category functional hierarchy across 1,594 genes
\item 60-fold clock gene enrichment in the resonance zone
\item 180-fold BMAL1 coupling enrichment over random predictors
\item 21 qualitative convergence points with independent research programs
\item ODE model dynamics from 5 canonical systems
\end{itemize}

If the underlying biology did not have a low-dimensional dynamical structure, two numbers could not capture all of this. The success of the minimal model is itself evidence that gene expression dynamics are simpler---more structured---than their apparent complexity suggests. This echoes the principle from systems biology that biological circuits often implement simple, robust motifs despite apparent molecular complexity \citep{alon2006introduction, stelling2004robustness}.

\section{Limitations}

This perspective piece has several important limitations that should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Scope as perspective:} This article presents an argument and a framework, not a comprehensive empirical study. The quantitative evidence is presented in detail in the companion method paper \citep{whiteside2026par2a}; here we focus on the conceptual case for temporal persistence as a biological property.

\item \textbf{Correlation, not causation:} The observation that eigenvalue recovers circadian hierarchy does not prove that eigenvalue \textit{causes} or \textit{explains} the hierarchy. The Bmal1-knockout causal validation \citep{whiteside2026par2a} provides the strongest causal evidence, but the cross-disciplinary analogies presented here are conceptual, not causal.

\item \textbf{AR(2) linearity assumption:} The AR(2) model assumes linear dynamics, which may not hold for genes subject to sharp thresholds, bistable switching, or strong nonlinear regulation. Nonlinear autoregressive extensions (e.g., SETAR, TAR models) could capture richer dynamics but at the cost of interpretability and increased parameter count.

\item \textbf{Limited circadian context:} The evidence presented is predominantly from circadian time-series data. Whether eigenvalue persistence generalizes to other temporal contexts---development, immune response, cell cycle---remains to be systematically tested. Preliminary cross-dataset results are encouraging but not definitive.

\item \textbf{Aging and disease claims are speculative:} The predictions about aging clocks and disease disruption (Section 6) are currently speculative extrapolations from circadian data. Dedicated aging cohort studies and disease-specific datasets are needed before these claims can be substantiated.

\item \textbf{Sampling resolution dependence:} The AR(2) eigenvalue depends on sampling interval. A gene sampled every 2 hours (GSE54650 \citep{zhang2014circadian}) and the same gene sampled every 4 hours will yield different $\phi_1$, $\phi_2$ coefficients, and thus different $|\lambda|$ values. Cross-dataset comparisons must account for this.

\item \textbf{Single-author perspective:} As an independent researcher, this work has not benefited from the iterative critique of a large laboratory group. The companion papers \citep{whiteside2026par2a} include extensive robustness testing to partially compensate.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We propose that temporal persistence---how much a gene's past determines its future---is a fundamental axis of biological variation, distinct from expression level, network connectivity, or epigenetic state. The AR(2) eigenvalue modulus provides a direct, interpretable measurement of this property using mathematics that has been refined across physics, engineering, economics \citep{sims1980macroeconomics, granger1969investigating}, and climate science \citep{hasselmann1976stochastic} over two centuries.

The eigenvalue is not new. What is new is recognizing that it applies to genes, and that the result is biologically meaningful. Gene expression is a dynamical system, and the universal language of dynamical systems---the eigenvalue---reads it just as well as it reads bridges, atoms, economies, and climate.
