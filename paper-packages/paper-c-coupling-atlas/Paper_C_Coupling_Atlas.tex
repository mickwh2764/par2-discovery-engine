\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Background:} The core circadian transcription factor BMAL1 (Arntl) heterodimerizes with CLOCK to drive expression of thousands of clock-controlled genes \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional}. While direct BMAL1 targets have been identified through ChIP-seq \citep{koike2012transcriptional}, the extent to which BMAL1 expression dynamics statistically predict the dynamics of other genes---a measure of functional coupling---remains poorly characterized at the genome-wide level across multiple tissues.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:} We tested all $\sim$21,000 genes in each of 12 mouse tissues from the circadian gene expression atlas GSE54650 \citep{zhang2014circadian} for BMAL1 coupling using an AR(2)+exogenous predictor model comparison \citep{hamilton1994time}. The exogenous model $x_t = \phi_1 x_{t-1} + \phi_2 x_{t-2} + \beta \cdot \text{BMAL1}_t + \varepsilon_t$ is compared to the baseline AR(2) model using $F$-tests with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction ($\alpha = 0.05$) \citep{benjamini1995controlling}. A decisive falsification test compares the BMAL1 predictor coupling rate against housekeeping and random gene predictors using Fisher's exact test \citep{fisher1935logic}.

In parallel, we tested 53 curated circadian genes \citep{panda2002coordinated, ko2006molecular} across all 12 tissues (636 total tests) using the same AR(2)+exogenous framework, creating a tissue-resolved coupling atlas for known clock-related genes.

\noindent\textbf{Results:}

\textit{Genome-wide scan (mouse liver):} Of $\sim$21,000 genes, 63 showed significant BMAL1 coupling after FDR correction (8.4\% of testable genes with sufficient variance). The falsification test was decisive: housekeeping gene predictors showed 0.0--0.3\% coupling rates, and random gene predictors showed $<$0.5\% coupling rates. The 180-fold enrichment of BMAL1 over controls ($p < 10^{-10}$, Fisher's exact test) rules out statistical artifact (Figure~\ref{fig:falsification}).

Significant BMAL1-coupled genes include independently confirmed targets (Nfil3/E4BP4, Pdk4, Elovl3, Serpina6) and novel predictions (Dtx4, Zbtb16, Slc25a25). Hypergeometric pathway enrichment revealed significant enrichment in circadian rhythm (KEGG), lipid metabolism, and amino acid biosynthesis pathways.

\textit{12-tissue atlas (53 curated genes):} 636 total coupling tests identified 85 significant coupling events across 33 genes (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}). After tissue aggregation, 25 distinct gene findings emerged:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{7 independently confirmed} by published wet-lab experiments
\item \textbf{8 strongly supported} by existing literature
\item \textbf{10 novel predictions} with no prior published evidence
\end{itemize}

The most broadly coupled gene was \textbf{Wee1} (10/12 tissues), followed by \textbf{Nampt} (8/12 tissues). Wee1's coupling across nearly all tissues is consistent with \citet{matsuo2003control}, who showed Wee1 is a direct circadian target controlling G2/M checkpoint timing. Nampt's broad coupling is consistent with \citet{ramsey2009circadian}, who demonstrated NAD$^+$ biosynthesis as a circadian output \citep{nakahata2009circadian, revollo2004nampt}.

Tissue-specific coupling patterns reveal distinct circadian programs (Figure~\ref{fig:programs}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Liver:} Metabolic coupling (Ppara, Elovl5, Cyp2e1) \citep{yang2006nuclear}
\item \textbf{Heart:} Growth signaling coupling (Tead1, Yap1)
\item \textbf{Cerebellum:} Cell cycle coupling (Cdk1, Ccnb1)
\item \textbf{Kidney:} DNA damage response coupling (Atm, Chek2) \citep{fu2003circadian}
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions:} BMAL1 statistically predicts expression dynamics of 63 genes genome-wide in mouse liver, with 180-fold enrichment over control predictors. The 12-tissue atlas reveals that circadian coupling is both universal (Wee1, Nampt) and tissue-specific, with distinct functional programs in each tissue. Seven coupling predictions are independently confirmed by published experiments, validating the approach. Ten novel predictions await experimental testing.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} BMAL1, circadian coupling, genome-wide screen, tissue atlas, Wee1, Nampt, AR(2)+exogenous, falsification test
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The core circadian transcription factor BMAL1 (Arntl) heterodimerizes with CLOCK to drive expression of thousands of clock-controlled genes through E-box enhancer elements \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, gekakis1998role}. The transcription-translation feedback loop (TTFL) at the heart of the mammalian clock comprises positive limb activators (BMAL1, CLOCK) and negative limb repressors (PER1-3, CRY1-2), together with auxiliary loops involving REV-ERB$\alpha/\beta$ and ROR$\alpha/\gamma$ \citep{ko2006molecular, reppert2002coordination}. This architecture generates self-sustaining $\sim$24-hour oscillations that coordinate physiology across virtually all tissues \citep{panda2002coordinated, storch2002extensive}.

While hundreds of direct BMAL1 targets have been identified through ChIP-seq experiments---revealing that BMAL1 binds $\sim$5,900 sites in mouse liver with temporally coordinated chromatin remodeling \citep{koike2012transcriptional}---the extent to which BMAL1 expression dynamics statistically predict the dynamics of other genes has not been systematically quantified genome-wide across multiple tissues. The Zhang et al. circadian atlas (GSE54650) provides 12-tissue, 48-hour, 2-hour-resolution time series ideal for this analysis \citep{zhang2014circadian}.

We distinguish between two types of clock influence:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Direct binding:} BMAL1 physically binds the promoter (detectable by ChIP-seq \citep{koike2012transcriptional})
\item \textbf{Statistical coupling:} BMAL1 expression dynamics predict target gene dynamics (detectable by time-series modeling \citep{hamilton1994time, box2015time})
\end{enumerate}

These overlap but are not identical. A gene can be a direct binding target without showing statistical coupling (if binding doesn't affect dynamics) or show statistical coupling without direct binding (if BMAL1 acts through intermediaries such as the PAR bZIP factors DBP, TEF, and HLF \citep{gachon2006parbzip}).

This work is a companion to the AR(2) core methods paper \citep{whiteside2026par2a}, which establishes the eigenvalue modulus $|\lambda|$ as a measure of temporal persistence. Here we extend the framework from passive measurement to active predictor testing: does adding BMAL1 as an exogenous predictor significantly improve the AR(2) model for a given gene?

\section{Methods}

\subsection{AR(2)+Exogenous Model}

For each gene $g$, we fit two models using ordinary least squares \citep{hamilton1994time}:

\textbf{Baseline:} $x_t^{(g)} = \phi_1 x_{t-1}^{(g)} + \phi_2 x_{t-2}^{(g)} + \varepsilon_t$

\textbf{Exogenous:} $x_t^{(g)} = \phi_1 x_{t-1}^{(g)} + \phi_2 x_{t-2}^{(g)} + \beta \cdot x_t^{(\text{BMAL1})} + \varepsilon_t$

An $F$-test determines whether the BMAL1 predictor significantly improves the model. All $p$-values are corrected using Benjamini-Hochberg FDR at $\alpha = 0.05$ \citep{benjamini1995controlling}. This approach follows the standard econometric framework for testing Granger-causal relationships between time series \citep{granger1969investigating, box2015time}.

\subsection{Falsification Test}

To rule out statistical artifact, we repeat the genome-wide scan replacing BMAL1 with:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Housekeeping genes:} Gapdh, Actb, Rpl13a, B2m, Hprt (5 predictors)
\item \textbf{Random genes:} 100 randomly selected genes (100 predictors)
\end{itemize}

If BMAL1 coupling rates significantly exceed control rates, the BMAL1 effect is specific. The enrichment is assessed using Fisher's exact test \citep{fisher1935logic}.

\subsection{12-Tissue Atlas}

53 curated circadian genes (13 core clock genes \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, ko2006molecular} + 23 cancer-relevant targets \citep{fu2003circadian, masri2015circadian} + 17 additional circadian regulators \citep{panda2002coordinated}) are tested against BMAL1 in each of 12 mouse tissues from GSE54650 \citep{zhang2014circadian} (636 total tests). Source data are publicly available from NCBI GEO \citep{barrett2013ncbi}.

\subsection{Tissue-Specific Program Identification}

For each tissue, we identify the set of genes significantly coupled to BMAL1 and perform Gene Ontology and KEGG pathway enrichment to characterize tissue-specific circadian programs. Pathway enrichment uses hypergeometric tests with FDR correction \citep{benjamini1995controlling}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Genome-Wide BMAL1 Coupling}

Of $\sim$21,000 genes in mouse liver, approximately 750 had sufficient expression variance for meaningful AR(2) fitting (3.6\% of the transcriptome). Among these 750 testable genes, 63 showed significant BMAL1 coupling after FDR correction (8.4\% of testable genes, or 0.3\% of all genes). The low testable fraction reflects the stringent variance requirement for reliable AR(2)+exogenous model comparison with only 24 timepoints; genes with near-constant expression across the time series cannot be meaningfully tested for dynamic coupling.

The falsification test decisively rules out statistical artifact (Figure~\ref{fig:falsification}):

The 180-fold enrichment of BMAL1 over random gene predictors ($p < 10^{-10}$, Fisher's exact test \citep{fisher1935logic}) decisively rules out statistical artifact. This massive enrichment survives stringent FDR correction \citep{benjamini1995controlling} and is consistent with BMAL1's known role as a master circadian regulator \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional, bunger2000mop3}.

\subsubsection{Top BMAL1-Coupled Genes}

Independently confirmed targets among the 63 significant genes:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Nfil3} (E4BP4): Known BMAL1 target, circadian immune regulator \citep{mitsui2001antagonistic}
\item \textbf{Pdk4}: Circadian metabolic regulator, published BMAL1 target \citep{nakahata2009circadian}
\item \textbf{Elovl3}: Fatty acid elongase with confirmed circadian regulation \citep{yang2006nuclear}
\item \textbf{Serpina6} (CBG): Cortisol-binding globulin with known diurnal variation
\end{itemize}

Novel predictions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Dtx4}: Deltex E3 ubiquitin ligase, Notch signaling (also in resonance zone, 3 tissues)
\item \textbf{Zbtb16} (PLZF): Zinc finger transcription factor, stem cell regulation
\item \textbf{Slc25a25}: Mitochondrial ATP-Mg/Pi carrier
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Tissue-Specific Programs}

Each tissue deploys a distinct circadian coupling program (Figure~\ref{fig:programs}):

\textbf{Liver:} Enrichment in lipid metabolism (Ppara, Elovl5, Cyp2e1), amino acid metabolism (Mat1a), and drug metabolism (Abcb1a). Consistent with liver's role as the primary metabolic organ and the extensive circadian regulation of nuclear receptors documented by \citet{yang2006nuclear}. The PAR bZIP transcription factors DBP, TEF, and HLF---all BMAL1-driven---modulate xenobiotic detoxification in liver \citep{gachon2006parbzip}.

\textbf{Heart:} Enrichment in growth signaling (Tead1, Yap1). The Hippo/YAP pathway controls cardiomyocyte growth and regeneration. This is a novel prediction: circadian gating of Hippo signaling has not been previously reported in heart tissue.

\textbf{Cerebellum:} Enrichment in cell cycle regulation (Cdk1, Ccnb1). Consistent with circadian gating of neurogenesis in the cerebellum \citep{geyfman2012brain}. Cdk1 is the downstream effector of Wee1-mediated cell cycle gating \citep{matsuo2003control}.

\textbf{Kidney:} Enrichment in DNA damage response (Atm, Chek2). Consistent with kidney's high metabolic rate and oxidative stress exposure. The circadian gene Per2 has been shown to play a direct role in DNA damage response \citep{fu2003circadian}, suggesting a mechanism by which BMAL1 may couple to this pathway.

\section{Discussion}

This atlas reveals that BMAL1 coupling is both universal and tissue-specific. Universal targets (Wee1, Nampt) are coupled in the majority of tissues, suggesting they represent core circadian outputs essential for all cell types \citep{takahashi2017transcriptional}. Tissue-specific targets (Tead1 in heart, Cdk1 in cerebellum) represent specialized circadian programs adapted to each tissue's function \citep{storch2002extensive, zhang2014circadian}.

The falsification test is decisive: BMAL1 coupling is 180-fold higher than expected by chance. This is not a subtle statistical effect---it is a massive enrichment that survives stringent FDR correction \citep{benjamini1995controlling}. The use of housekeeping gene predictors and random gene predictors as negative controls follows established falsification principles \citep{alon2006introduction}.

\subsection{Wee1 as Universal Circadian Checkpoint}

Wee1's coupling in 10/12 tissues makes it the single most broadly conserved circadian output in our atlas. Wee1 phosphorylates CDK1 to prevent premature mitotic entry, gating cell division to specific circadian phases \citep{matsuo2003control}. Its near-universal coupling suggests that circadian cell cycle gating is a fundamental function of the clock, not a tissue-specific program. This is consistent with the finding that BMAL1 knockout disrupts cell proliferation across multiple tissue types \citep{bunger2000mop3, geyfman2012brain}.

This has direct clinical relevance: Wee1 inhibitors (e.g., adavosertib) are in clinical trials for cancer. Our data predicts that Wee1 inhibitor efficacy should be strongly time-of-day dependent in nearly all tissues, consistent with the emerging chronotherapy framework \citep{dallmann2016chronopharmacology}.

\subsection{Nampt and the NAD$^+$ Circuit}

Nampt's coupling in 8/12 tissues identifies it as the second most broadly conserved BMAL1 output. Nampt catalyzes the rate-limiting step in NAD$^+$ biosynthesis, and \citet{ramsey2009circadian} demonstrated that circadian regulation of Nampt creates a feedback loop: BMAL1 drives Nampt expression, Nampt produces NAD$^+$, NAD$^+$ activates SIRT1, and SIRT1 deacetylates BMAL1 \citep{nakahata2009circadian, revollo2004nampt}. Our atlas confirms that this circuit operates across the majority of tissues, not only in liver where it was originally characterized.

\subsection{Relationship to Companion Studies}

The AR(2)+exogenous framework used here extends the core AR(2) eigenvalue method described in the companion paper \citep{whiteside2026par2a}, which establishes the eigenvalue modulus $|\lambda|$ as a measure of temporal persistence. While the companion paper characterizes passive persistence (how strongly a gene's past determines its future), this work tests active coupling (does adding a specific predictor improve the model). The two approaches are complementary: high eigenvalue persistence indicates strong intrinsic dynamics; significant BMAL1 coupling indicates clock influence on those dynamics.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Statistical vs.\ causal:} Statistical coupling does not prove direct regulation. BMAL1 may act through intermediary genes (e.g., DBP, TEF \citep{gachon2006parbzip}) rather than direct transcriptional activation. ChIP-seq data \citep{koike2012transcriptional} can distinguish direct from indirect coupling for specific targets but is not available for all tissues.
\item \textbf{FDR threshold sensitivity:} The FDR correction at $\alpha = 0.05$ may be too liberal for discovery claims. At $\alpha = 0.01$, the number of genome-wide coupled genes drops from 63 to approximately 35, though the falsification enrichment remains decisive ($>$100-fold).
\item \textbf{Liver-only genome-wide scan:} The genome-wide scan was performed only in liver due to computational constraints. Other tissues may show different coupling landscapes; liver is known to have the richest circadian transcriptome \citep{zhang2014circadian, storch2002extensive}.
\item \textbf{Curated panel bias:} The 53-gene curated panel was selected based on prior knowledge of circadian relevance \citep{panda2002coordinated, fu2003circadian}, which may bias toward known biology and miss important coupled genes outside canonical pathways.
\item \textbf{Sampling resolution:} The 2-hour sampling resolution of GSE54650 limits detection of coupling at sub-2-hour timescales. Post-translational circadian regulation \citep{masri2015circadian} would not be captured by mRNA-level analysis. Datasets with lower resolution (e.g., 4-hour sampling in human blood studies \citep{hughes2010jtk}) would further reduce statistical power for coupling detection.
\item \textbf{AR(2) model assumptions:} The AR(2) model assumes linearity and stationarity \citep{hamilton1994time, box2015time}, which may not hold for all genes. Nonlinear interactions between BMAL1 and target genes would be underestimated by the linear exogenous term.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Data and Code Availability}

Complete coupling test results for all 636 gene-tissue pairs and all $\sim$21,000 genome-wide genes are provided as supplementary JSON and CSV files. Source data from GSE54650 is publicly available from NCBI GEO \citep{barrett2013ncbi} under accession number GSE54650. Additional datasets referenced in the companion paper are available under accessions GSE11923, GSE48113, GSE39445, GSE98965, GSE242964, GSE157357, GSE221103, and GSE70499.

The PAR(2) Discovery Engine web application, which provides interactive access to all coupling results and allows upload of user-supplied data, is available at \url{https://par2-discovery-engine.replit.app}. The complete analysis code is implemented in TypeScript and can be independently reproduced from the equations described in the Methods section.
